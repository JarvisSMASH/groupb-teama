Energy Inequality

A lack of adequate, reliable and affordable supplies of modern energy limits productive opportunities, enterprise growth and employment, and has a negative impact on human health and welfare.
Globally 3.5 billion people lack proper energy access, and 1.2 billion are children.
About half the children in the developing world attend schools without electricity.
Some 1 billion people receive substandard healthcare because of a lack of electricity.
The global population is expanding by more than 200,000 people each day, and by 2050, the worldâ€™s population is forecast to exceed 9.6 billion, with over two-thirds living in cities.
In some countries, fewer than half of all health facilities have access to electricity, and access levels are lower in rural areas. In Africa, just 35% of primary schools have electricity access, compared with 48% in South Asia and 93% in Latin America.

Energy bills represent approximately one-fifth of low-income Americans' budgets, disproportionately affecting black and Hispanic communities.

Energy Poverty in Metro Atlanta

According to a 2011 National Energy Assistance Directors poll of low-income families, 24 percent went without food for a day and 37 percent forwent medical and dental coverage to pay for higher energy bills. Nearly 1 in 5 had a family member become sick because their home was too cold.

Around the world, 1.3 billion people are without dependable power. More than 2.5 billion people use animal waste, leaves, wood, and charcoal to cook their food and heat their homes.
Energy poverty has become so severe in the Southeast that many households pay 600 percent more of their annual income on energy than the national average. In Memphis, the community is banding together to fight back.
This problem is especially stark in the Southeast, where many American families are faced with the decision of whether they must heat their homes sufficiently or forgo basic needs such as medicine and meals, simply to be able to pay the fluctuating monthly expenses of energy. The disparity is worse financially: While the average family pays an average of 3 percent of their income on energy, low-income families living in energy poverty are paying upwards of 20 percent of their income on energy expenses.
